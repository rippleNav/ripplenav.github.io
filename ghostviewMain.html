The Ghostview Experiments

An exploration into visualizing the potential influence of consciousness on random data. This project began with a proposal by a mathematician and evolved through three distinct software experiments, each with a different approach to revealing patterns within pseudorandomness.

Experiment 1: Cumulative Random Modifications

This is the original concept, based directly on the initial proposal. It operates by making thousands of small, random changes to a canvasâ€”adding or subtracting color from pixels, drawing tiny triangles, and shifting small blocks. The theory is that a persistent, subtle influence could slowly "build" a coherent image out of the cumulative chaos. This is a slow, painterly process.

Experiment 2: Visualizing Statistical Deviation

The second iteration takes a more analytical approach. It visualizes the real-time statistical deviation of a short-term average of random numbers from a long-term baseline. The color of each pixel represents this deviation. The image is "painted" over time, creating a historical map of the data stream's behavior. This model introduced the idea of a tunable "Sensitivity" to find signals in the noise.

Experiment 3: Real-time Modulated Video

The final and most advanced version. This model abandons the slow, cumulative approach for a fully real-time "video feed." On every frame, a new field of random noise is generated and then blended with the previous frame using temporal smoothing (Lerp). This creates a fluid, cloud-like medium designed for immediate, biofeedback-style response. This is the ideal instrument for observing coherent, moving forms that might emerge and fade from moment to moment.
