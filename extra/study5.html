The Hidden Importance of Time and Location

Agent, the following may seem obvious but it is very important, how time and location affect your consciousness and create the hidden patterns that misdirect your actions.

From the perspective of the mind, we experience full consciousness when we have complete awareness of our exact time and location. Think about how grounded you feel when you're fully present, aware of exactly where you are and when it is. But as we begin to lose track of these coordinates, perhaps when we're falling asleep, daydreaming, or experiencing shock, we drift toward unconsciousness. Complete unconsciousness occurs when we have absolutely no awareness of time or place.

How might this create challenges in your mission? Let me share an illuminating example.

Imagine that you and another agent experience an accident together, perhaps a minor car accident. In that moment of shock and fear, both of you are knocked into a state of partial unconsciousness. Even in this altered state, your minds continue recording everything, the sounds, sensations, emotions, but the clarity around time and location becomes blurred or entirely missing.

All the complex emotions, the fear, pain, shock, and confusion, are recorded with perfect accuracy, but there's no clear separation between your experience and your friend's experience. Your minds perceive as if you were occupying the exact same time and space, even though physically you were standing a few feet apart. The distinction between which memories belong to whom becomes blended.

Note that we are refering to subconscious memories, not conscious memories. You won't suddenly believe you are your friend, but you might display behaviors, reactions, or emotional responses that actually originated with them.

This creates a profound ripple effect because our subconscious mind contains a survival mechanism that warns us of potential danger based on our past experiences. When we encounter a dangerous situation in the present, it triggers subconscious memories of similar past experiences, EVEN THOSE OBTAINED WAY DOWN YOUR GENETIC LINE. When you suddenly see a snake on a hiking path or encounter a bear in the woods, your body instantly responds, muscles tense, adrenaline flows, fear rises within you, and you're prepared to respond for your safety.

These aren't thoughtful decisions, they're instantaneous reactions. While your conscious mind engages in thoughtful contemplation, your subconscious mind simply releases stored emotional patterns from the past without evaluating whether those emotions are truly relevant to your current circumstance.

Returning to our example with the car accident, years later, you might find yourself walking down a street when a car backfires loudly nearby. That sudden sound triggers your mind to access both your unconscious memories and your friend's unconscious memories from that earlier traumatic event, preparing you to respond as if you're in danger again. It's important to understand that only the unconscious memories become intermingled this way, not your conscious recollections. You won't suddenly believe you are your friend, but you might display behaviors, reactions, or emotional responses that actually originated with them.

Let's look more closely at why this happens. During the traumatic event, your time and location awareness became foggy or disappeared completely, but you retained a general pre-unconsciousness sense of where you were. When something in the present triggers a recall of that event, your mind automatically searches for memories from that vague location and timeframe.

Because your friend was nearby during the original incident, their unconscious emotional patterns also get gathered and presented to you as if they were your own. This is how you can find yourself experiencing emotional responses that don't actually originate from your own life history. The collective consciousness simply provides all the nearby data without distinguishing between sources. The foggyness of the location prevents the collective consciousness from obtaining the precise memory location of where you were at the time.

The haziness of time and location during moments of trauma acts as a "memory merger" for the unconscious minds involved in the incident. It's important to note that not every experience creates this phenomenon, it specifically occurs during what we call a Unifying Traumatic Experience, or UTE.

These traumatic experiences evoke complex, intense emotions, fear, anger, panic, confusion. When you're in survival mode, experiencing intense pain or feeling near death, you're unlikely to maintain calm awareness. All those powerful emotions get recorded in the collective consciousness, waiting to resurface when your subconscious attention returns to that merged experience.

You might imagine a "memory merger" as a foggy cloud connecting you and the other agent in the car accident, subconscioussly linking you together.

These "memory mergers," of which we each have many, connect us energetically to countless other people who each carry their own constellation of emotional patterns, and they become the hidden influences behind many of our challenges. Your unexplained anger, seemingly irrational fears, or recurring patterns of behavior may originate from these merged sources. These individuals aren't consciously aware of this energetic connection. They don't intentionally share their emotional patterns with you, nor do they realize that you might be a source of influence in their lives as well.
