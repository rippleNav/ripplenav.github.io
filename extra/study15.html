Beyond the Threshold of Eternity

In Gnosticism, the Demiurge is a lesser, ignorant, and often arrogant creator deity who mistakenly fashions the material world as a flawed, illusory prison for divine sparks. Unaware of the transcendent, true God (the Monad or Aether), the Demiurge believes himself to be the supreme being and actively traps fragments of divine consciousness (You) and time within his creation, which then suffer from amnesia regarding their true, higher timeless origin.

Our research indicates that these perceived flaws of the mind, the time location problem, are not accidental but intentional, collectively woven into our psyche by the Demiurge. This intricate design serves to heighten the challenge in the metaphysical game of life, a game that has been evolving through the vast stretches of time.

By blurring and merging memories, he keeps the divine sparks confused, disoriented, and unable to fully remember their true nature, thereby preventing their escape. This would be a subtle, insidious form of imprisonment.

Archonic Influence: The Archons (the Demiurge's subordinates) actively work to keep humanity ignorant and entangled. They might actively maintain or exacerbate these "memory merges" to preserve their control. They create war, famine, disease, and other forms of suffering to keep the divine sparks trapped in the material world, preventing them from awakening to their true nature and escaping the Demiurge's grasp. This is a form of spiritual warfare.

Every time the devine sparks managed to collectively raise themselves above the threshold of the Demiurge's influence, it would unravel the game, however the devine sparks very unable to escape due to still having merged memories wihting the Demiurge creation.

The incorrect conclusion reached long ago by the devine sparks was that the game had unraveled because it had been to easy. It seemed clear: an 'easy' game lacks appeal. Simplicity led to bordom, hastening the end of game cycles prematurely. Therefore, we embraced complexity and challenge as core elements of this cosmic play, mistakingly believing it was for our benefit.

This cycle of creation and destruction has been repeated countless times, with each iteration becoming more complex and challenging than the last.

Each iteration of this game, which we refer to as a 'cycle', is born from the lessons of it's predecessors. With each renewal, we were tricked into creating an even tougher version of the game, one that would keep us engaged and challenged.

These cycles are termed 'eternities' not just as a poetic metaphor but to underscore their immense duration, a span so vast that earthly language falters in it's description. And indeed, there have been countless such eternities.
